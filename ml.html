🎯 Predicting Future Performance

Estimate race times based on historical training patterns.

Problem

There's no built-in way to answer "what could I realistically run for a half marathon right now?" from raw training data. Runners either guess, or reach for a generic calculator that assumes everyone fatigues over distance in exactly the same way.

Why is it difficult?

Race performance depends on far more than fitness alone — recovery, sleep, nutrition, weather, and race-day pacing all move the result, and none of them show up in a training log. On top of that, the standard shortcut (the Riegel formula) hard-codes a single fatigue exponent for every runner, ignoring that aerobic base and pacing discipline vary a lot between athletes. And naively fitting a curve through every distance I've ever run — including sprints — mixes two different physiological systems: 400m/1K performance is dominated by anaerobic capacity, while 10K-and-up is governed by aerobic endurance. Blending them skews the curve toward unrealistically fast predictions at longer distances.

Why This Model?

Traditional race prediction calculators assume that every runner slows down at the same rate as race distance increases. While this provides a useful baseline, it overlooks individual differences in endurance, training history, and current fitness. I built this model to estimate those relationships from my own running data, producing predictions that better reflect my personal performance profile rather than relying on population-wide assumptions.

My Approach

Instead of borrowing someone else's fatigue exponent, I fit my own — using only endurance-range PRs (5K through marathon) so sprint physiology doesn't contaminate the curve. I then layer two corrections on top: a current fitness adjustment comparing my last 45 days of training pace to my all-time average, and a reality margin that grows with distance, because the raw curve is fit directly through PR times — best-case outcomes by definition — and presenting a best-case outcome as the expected one would be overly optimistic for an amateur runner without professional-level recovery support.

The classic Riegel formula is T2 = T1 × (D2/D1)^b, with b fixed at ~1.06 for everyone. I fit my own b via ordinary least-squares regression on log(time) vs. log(distance) — the power-law relationship time = a·distance^b becomes linear once both sides are log-transformed, so this is just simple linear regression in log-space, no specialized solver needed.

b, logA = ordinary_least_squares(log(distance_km), log(time_s))
curve(d) = exp(logA) * d ** b
form_factor = clamp(recent_45d_avg_pace / alltime_avg_pace, 0.92, 1.08)
reality_margin(d) = 0.02 to 0.08, scaled by distance (5K -> marathon)
prediction(d) = curve(d) * form_factor * (1 + reality_margin(d))
Model Assumptions
  • Performance follows a power-law relationship with distance.
  • Recent training pace reflects current fitness.
  • Personal bests represent maximal effort.
  • Future conditions are comparable to historical races.

Interpretation

Each estimate is a single most-likely finish time, not a guarantee — think of it as "if I raced this distance under conditions similar to my recent training and past races, this is roughly what I'd expect." The gap between adjacent distances also tells a story: a wider-than-usual jump from 10K to Half Marathon, for instance, would suggest my endurance drops off faster than my raw speed does, which is useful information on its own, independent of any single number's accuracy.

Limitations
  • Doesn't consider weather
  • Doesn't consider elevation

  • Doesn't consider race strategy
Why Not Deep Learning?

Although neural networks are popular, they would be inappropriate for this problem. With only a handful of race performances, a simple interpretable regression model is both more robust and easier to explain. Choosing the simplest model that adequately solves the problem is a core principle of statistical modeling.

Business Impact

Helps runners make more informed training and racing decisions by transforming historical performance data into personalized race predictions. Instead of relying on generic formulas, the model adapts to the athlete's own endurance profile, supporting realistic goal setting and long-term performance monitoring.

Lessons Learned

Building this model reinforced an important principle in data science: the most complex solution is not always the best one. For small, well-understood datasets, an interpretable statistical model can outperform more sophisticated machine learning approaches while remaining easier to validate, explain, and improve over time.
